Detailed Findings

Comprehensive results from the trauma access analysis

1. Trauma Desert Identification

Using bivariate classification of shooting density and transport time terciles, we identified 18 census tracts (4.4% of the city) as trauma deserts. These tracts have both high violence burden and extended transport times to Level I trauma care.

Metric Value
Total trauma desert tracts 18 of 408 (4.4%)
Population in trauma deserts 83,159 residents (5.2% of city)
Shootings in trauma deserts 1,659 (9.5% of total)
Average transport time (trauma deserts) 12.5 minutes
Average transport time (other tracts) 11.3 minutes

2. Golden Hour Coverage

Despite identifying trauma desert tracts, Philadelphia's overall geographic access to Level I trauma care is excellent. 99.6% of all shootings occurred within 20 minutes of a Level I trauma center.

Transport Time Shootings Percentage
0-5 minutes 6,319 36.4%
5-10 minutes 9,405 54.1%
10-15 minutes 1,334 7.7%
15-20 minutes 260 1.5%
20+ minutes 62 0.4%

This finding suggests that the "trauma desert" phenomenon in Philadelphia is driven primarily by violence concentration, not by poor geographic access to trauma care.

3. Demographic Disparities

Trauma deserts are disproportionately located in predominantly Black and high-poverty neighborhoods.

Metric Trauma Deserts Other Tracts p-value
Mean % Black 76.0% 38.3% <0.0001
Mean % Poverty 30.8% 21.8% 0.0009

However, a counterintuitive finding emerges: predominantly Black neighborhoods are actually closer to Level I trauma centers on average. The correlation between percent Black and transport time is negative (r = -0.25, p < 0.0001).

4. Oaxaca-Blinder Decomposition

To rigorously quantify the racial disparity, we applied Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition to separate the gap between predominantly Black tracts and other tracts.

Shooting Density Gap

Predominantly Black tracts experience 4.4x higher shooting density (geometric mean ratio). The decomposition reveals:

  • 31.5% Explained: Attributable to differences in poverty rates and median income
  • 68.5% Unexplained: Residual gap that cannot be attributed to observable socioeconomic characteristics

The large unexplained portion suggests structural and historical factors beyond current socioeconomic conditions contribute to the concentration of violence.

Transport Time Gap

Black tracts are 3.2 minutes closer to Level I trauma centers on average. This finding confirms that the disparity in trauma burden is not compounded by geographic access barriers.

6. Temporal Patterns

Shooting incidents exhibited substantial temporal variation over the study period, with a pronounced spike during 2020-2021.

Period Annual Average Change from Baseline
2015-2019 (Pre-COVID baseline) 1,361 --
2020-2021 (Pandemic peak) 2,297 +68.8%
2022-2025 (Post-peak) 1,495 +9.8%

Peak year was 2021 with 2,338 shootings. Violence has since declined but remains elevated compared to pre-pandemic levels.

7. Vulnerability Index

We constructed a Neighborhood Vulnerability Index (NVI) combining poverty rate, household income, shooting density, and transport time. The index examines whether trauma deserts coincide with other forms of structural disadvantage.

  • City average vulnerability score: 16.5
  • Trauma desert average: 21.6 (1.31x higher)
  • 9 of 18 trauma deserts (50%) are in the top quartile of vulnerability

8. Policy Implications

Stop the Bleed Training Prioritization

We identified 20 priority zones for community hemorrhage control training based on a composite score of shooting density and transport time. The top 20 zones contain 126,586 residents and experienced 2,607 shootings over the study period.

Conservative estimates suggest community-based hemorrhage control training in these zones could potentially save approximately 7 lives annually.

Scenario Modeling

Hypothetical placement of new trauma facilities was modeled at 8 candidate locations. Key findings (using approximate distance-based travel time estimates):

  • Hunting Park: Highest overall impact score (would improve access for 198,932 residents)
  • Kingsessing/Cobbs Creek: Would help the most trauma desert tracts (8 of 18)
  • Frankford/Mayfair: Would reach the largest population (323,391)
