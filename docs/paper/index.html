The Trauma Desert: Spatial Mismatch Between Gun Violence Burden and Level I Trauma Access in Philadelphia

A Geospatial Analysis of 17,380 Shooting Incidents, 2015-2025

Yoel Y. Plutchok

January 2026

Abstract

Background: The "golden hour" principle in trauma care establishes that survival rates for penetrating injuries decline significantly when definitive surgical care is delayed. This study examines whether Philadelphia neighborhoods with the highest gun violence burden face systematic barriers to timely Level I trauma care.

Methods: We analyzed 17,380 shooting incidents in Philadelphia (2015-2025) using bivariate classification of shooting density and modeled transport time to the nearest Level I Adult trauma center. We tested the association between transport time and fatality using logistic regression.

Results: We identified 18 census tracts (4.4%) as trauma deserts, yet 99.6% of all shootings occurred within 20 minutes of Level I trauma care. Predominantly Black tracts were actually 3.2 minutes closer to trauma centers but experienced 4.4-fold higher shooting density. Fatality rates increased from 20.0% (0-5 min) to 30.6% (20+ min) with transport time (OR=1.015/min, p=0.005).

Conclusions: The trauma desert phenomenon is driven by violence concentration rather than geographic access barriers. While transport time affects mortality, the disparity in trauma burden—not distance to care—represents the primary equity concern.

Keywords: trauma systems, gun violence, health disparities, geographic access, golden hour, penetrating trauma, Philadelphia

Introduction

The Golden Hour and Trauma Care Access

The concept of the "golden hour" has guided trauma system development since its articulation by R. Adams Cowley. For penetrating trauma such as gunshot wounds, the critical window may be even narrower—the first 10-20 minutes are essential for hemorrhage control and definitive surgical intervention. Level I trauma centers provide the highest capability for treating severe injuries, with 24/7 surgical coverage and comprehensive resources.

Geographic access to trauma care has emerged as a significant determinant of outcomes. Research has demonstrated that access to Level I trauma centers within 60 minutes significantly reduces mortality for severely injured patients, and that patients transported directly to Level I centers have substantially lower mortality than those taken to non-trauma facilities.

Gun Violence as a Public Health Crisis

Philadelphia experiences among the highest rates of gun violence of any major American city. Between 2015 and 2025, the city recorded over 17,000 shooting incidents, with a pronounced spike during 2020-2021. Gun violence is heavily concentrated in specific neighborhoods that also experience other forms of structural disadvantage.

Research Question

Is there a spatial mismatch between gun violence burden and timely access to Level I trauma care in Philadelphia, and does this mismatch disproportionately affect predominantly Black and low-income communities?

Methods

Study Design and Data Sources

We conducted a retrospective geospatial analysis of 17,380 shooting incidents in Philadelphia from January 2015 through December 2025. Shooting data was obtained from OpenDataPhilly. Trauma center locations were verified through the Pennsylvania Trauma Systems Foundation. Drive-time isochrones were generated via the OpenRouteService API. Census tract demographics were obtained from the American Community Survey.

Bivariate Classification

Tracts were classified using a 3×3 matrix combining shooting density terciles and transport time terciles. "Trauma deserts" were defined as Class 9 tracts—those in the highest tercile for both dimensions.

Discussion

Principal Findings

This study reveals a paradox: while 18 census tracts meet our criteria for trauma desert status, the primary driver of elevated trauma burden is violence concentration, not geographic access barriers.

Three key findings support this interpretation:

  1. Excellent geographic coverage: 99.6% of shootings occur within 20 minutes of Level I trauma care.
  2. Inverse access-race relationship: Predominantly Black neighborhoods are closer to trauma centers, not farther.
  3. Violence concentration: The 4.4-fold disparity in shooting density is largely unexplained by socioeconomic factors.

Transport Time Still Matters

Our finding that fatality rates increase from 20.0% to 30.6% across the transport time gradient confirms clinical relevance. However, because so few shootings occur in high-transport-time areas, improving geographic access alone would have limited population-level impact.

Policy Implications

Traditional approaches—building new facilities or redistributing EMS resources—would have limited impact given excellent existing coverage. Interventions should focus on:

  • Violence prevention: Addressing root causes through community-based intervention programs
  • Community hemorrhage control: Expanding "Stop the Bleed" training in high-burden neighborhoods
  • Hospital capacity: Ensuring Temple and Penn Presbyterian have adequate resources

Conclusion

The trauma desert phenomenon in Philadelphia is driven primarily by extreme concentration of gun violence rather than geographic access barriers. While transport time significantly affects mortality, the 4.4-fold disparity in shooting density—68.5% unexplained by socioeconomic factors—represents a fundamental health equity crisis that cannot be addressed through trauma system redesign alone. Reducing gun violence burden in affected neighborhoods must be the primary policy objective.
