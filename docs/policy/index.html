Gun Violence and Trauma Access in Philadelphia

Findings and Recommendations for Reducing Preventable Deaths

Prepared by Yoel Y. Plutchok | January 2026

Executive Summary

This report presents findings from a comprehensive geospatial analysis of gun violence and trauma care access in Philadelphia. Using 11 years of shooting data (2015-2025) and drive-time modeling to Level I trauma centers, we identify neighborhoods facing compounded disadvantage and provide evidence-based recommendations for reducing preventable deaths.

18 "Trauma desert" neighborhoods identified
99.6% of shootings within 20 min of Level I trauma
54.9% of shootings in Temple Hospital's catchment
4.4x higher shooting rate in Black neighborhoods
The disparity is in VIOLENCE, not ACCESS

Black neighborhoods are actually 3.2 minutes CLOSER to trauma centers, yet experience 4.4x higher shooting rates

The Problem

Philadelphia has experienced a gun violence crisis for decades, with over 17,000 shooting incidents between 2015 and 2025. A "trauma desert" is a neighborhood that faces two compounding disadvantages: high gun violence burden AND poor access to trauma care.

Key Findings

Finding 1: Geographic Access is Actually GOOD

Contrary to expectations, Philadelphia has excellent coverage:

Within 10 minutes90.5%

Implication: Building new trauma centers would have limited impact because access is already good for 99.6% of victims.

Recommendations

Recommendation 1: Violence Prevention Investment

Target: City Council, Mayor's Office, Department of Public Health

The 18 trauma desert tracts should receive priority funding for community violence intervention programs, youth employment, conflict mediation, and mental health services.

Recommendation 2: Expand Community Hemorrhage Control

Target: Fire Department, Public Health, Community Organizations

Deploy "Stop the Bleed" training in priority neighborhoods. Target sites: schools, SEPTA stations, recreation centers, churches. Estimated impact: ~7 lives saved annually in top 20 zones.

Recommendation 3: Hospital Capacity Assurance

Target: Temple University Hospital, Penn Presbyterian, Health System Leadership

Ensure hospitals bearing the greatest burden have adequate trauma bay capacity, staffing, surge capability, and staff wellness support.

Recommendation 4: Data-Driven Resource Allocation

Target: Police, Fire, Managing Director's Office

Use geographic analysis to position EMS units, pre-stage resources during high-risk periods, and monitor emerging hotspots.

Recommendation 5: Address Root Causes

Target: City Council, State Legislature, Federal Partners

The 68.5% unexplained disparity points to structural factors. Long-term solutions require equitable development, housing stability, educational investment, and reparative justice.
