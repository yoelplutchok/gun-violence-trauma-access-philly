Research Methodology

Technical approach to measuring trauma access disparities

Research Purpose

This study addresses a fundamental public health question: Is there a spatial mismatch between gun violence burden and timely access to Level I trauma care in Philadelphia? The "golden hour" principle in trauma care establishes that survival rates decline significantly when definitive surgical care is delayed beyond 60 minutes post-injury. For penetrating trauma such as gunshot wounds, the first 10-20 minutes are particularly critical for hemorrhage control.

We operationalize "trauma desert" status through bivariate classification, identifying census tracts that simultaneously experience high violence burden and extended transport times to Level I trauma centers. This approach moves beyond simple proximity analysis to capture the compounding disadvantage of both exposure and access barriers.

Data Sources

Shooting Incident Data

Shooting victim data was obtained from OpenDataPhilly, the City of Philadelphia's open data portal. The dataset includes all reported shooting incidents from January 2015 through December 2025, with geocoded coordinates, victim demographics, and outcome information. After removing records with invalid coordinates, the analysis includes 17,383 shooting incidents.

Trauma Center Locations

Level I and Level II trauma centers were identified from the Pennsylvania Trauma Systems Foundation (PTSF) accreditation registry and verified against the American College of Surgeons trauma center verification database. Philadelphia contains four Level I Adult trauma centers (Temple University Hospital, Penn Presbyterian Medical Center, Thomas Jefferson University Hospital, and Jefferson Einstein Philadelphia Hospital), two Level I Pediatric centers, and one Level II Adult center.

Drive-Time Isochrones

Travel time polygons were generated using the OpenRouteService API with driving-car profile. For each Level I Adult trauma center, isochrones were computed at 5, 10, 15, 20, and 30 minute intervals. These represent the geographic area reachable within each time threshold under typical traffic conditions.

Census Geography and Demographics

Census tract boundaries were obtained from the U.S. Census Bureau TIGER/Line files. Demographic data including population, racial composition, and poverty rates were obtained from the American Community Survey 5-year estimates. Philadelphia County contains 408 census tracts.

Analytical Methods

Spatial Assignment

Each shooting incident was assigned to a census tract using point-in-polygon spatial join. Of 17,383 cleaned records, 17,380 were successfully assigned to tracts; 3 records with coordinates outside Philadelphia tract boundaries were excluded from tract-based analyses. Tract centroids were computed and used to determine transport time categories based on isochrone containment. Transport time to the nearest Level I Adult trauma center was categorized into discrete intervals (0-5, 5-10, 10-15, 15-20, 20-30, 30+ minutes).

Shooting Density Calculation

For each tract, shooting density was calculated as the total number of shootings divided by tract area (in square miles) divided by the number of years in the study period (11 years). This produces a standardized rate of shootings per square mile per year, enabling comparison across tracts of varying size.

Bivariate Classification

Tracts were classified using a 3x3 bivariate matrix combining shooting density terciles and transport time terciles. Tercile thresholds were computed using all 408 tracts:

  • Shooting Density: Low (bottom third), Medium (middle third), High (top third)
  • Transport Time: Low/Good (bottom third), Medium (middle third), High/Poor (top third)

The resulting 9-class system identifies "trauma deserts" as Class 9 tracts with both high shooting density AND high transport time.

Statistical Testing

Demographic comparisons between trauma desert and non-trauma desert tracts were conducted using Welch's t-test (unequal variance). Correlations between variables were assessed using Pearson's r. All tests used alpha = 0.05 for significance.

Oaxaca-Blinder Decomposition

To quantify the racial disparity in violence burden, we applied Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition separating tracts into predominantly Black (50%+ Black population, n=143) and other tracts (n=246). The decomposition separates the gap into:

  • Explained portion: Attributable to differences in observed characteristics (poverty rate, median income)
  • Unexplained portion: Residual gap not explained by observable factors

Shooting density was log-transformed (log1p) for the decomposition to address skewness. The reported ratio (4.4x) represents the geometric mean ratio exp(mean log density difference).

Fatality-Transport Analysis

To validate the theoretical basis for the trauma desert framework, we tested whether transport time is associated with fatality outcomes in this dataset. Using the binary fatality indicator in the shooting data:

  • Chi-square test: Association between transport time category and fatality (5 categories)
  • Logistic regression: Fatality ~ Transport Time (minutes), with odds ratio per minute

This analysis provides within-dataset evidence supporting the clinical relevance of transport time, complementing the extensive published literature on trauma transport and mortality.

Literature Foundation

The relationship between transport time and survival for penetrating trauma is well-established:

  • Newgard CD et al. (2010). "The Effect of Transport Time on Mortality for Trauma Patients." Annals of Emergency Medicine. Found increased mortality with transport times >45 min.
  • Byrne JP et al. (2019). "Association Between Emergency Medical Service Response Time and Mortality in Penetrating Trauma." JAMA Surgery. Shorter times improved outcomes.
  • Wandling M et al. (2016). "Impact of Level I Trauma Center Access on Mortality." JAMA Surgery. Access within 60 min reduced mortality.
  • Brown JB et al. (2016). "Not All Prehospital Time Is Equal: The Impact of Transport Destination on Outcomes." Annals of Surgery. Level I center transport improved survival.

Limitations

Limitation Description Mitigation
Transport time approximation Isochrones assume typical traffic conditions; actual transport varies by time of day Sensitivity analysis showed classification stable across rush hour scenarios
Centroid assumption Using tract centroids ignores within-tract variation in access Most tracts are small enough that centroid is representative
Hospital catchment modeling Nearest-hospital assignment does not reflect actual EMS destination decisions Clearly labeled as "modeled catchment" throughout
No prehospital data Analysis does not include EMS response times or scene times Focus is on transport time component only
Discrete time buckets 5-minute intervals create artificial boundaries Tercile classification reduces sensitivity to exact cutoffs

Reproducibility

All analysis code is available in the GitHub repository . The analysis pipeline can be reproduced using the provided Makefile: 

make setup      # Install dependencies
make collect    # Download raw data
make isochrones # Generate drive-time polygons (requires API key)
make process    # Clean and transform data
make analyze    # Run statistical analysis
make visualize  # Generate maps and figures
make test       # Validation checks
