Gun Violence Burden and Trauma System Capacity in Philadelphia

A Geospatial Analysis of Access Disparities, 2015-2025

Executive Summary

This study examines the spatial relationship between gun violence and access to Level I trauma care in Philadelphia. Using 17,383 shooting incidents over 11 years and drive-time isochrones from trauma centers, we identify 18 census tracts that qualify as "trauma deserts" - areas with both high violence burden and extended transport times to definitive care.

Critical finding: The primary driver of trauma burden disparity is the extreme concentration of gun violence in certain neighborhoods, not geographic barriers to trauma care. Black neighborhoods are actually closer to Level I trauma centers on average, but experience dramatically higher rates of shootings.

Principal Findings

Violence Concentration

Predominantly Black tracts (50%+ Black population) experience 4.4x higher shooting density than other tracts. This disparity persists after controlling for poverty and income - 68.5% remains unexplained by observable characteristics.

Geographic Access

Contrary to expectations, Black neighborhoods are 3.2 minutes closer to Level I trauma centers on average. The "trauma desert" problem is driven by violence burden, not healthcare access barriers.

Hospital Burden

Temple University Hospital serves as the nearest Level I center for 54.9% of shooting locations. Combined with Penn Presbyterian, two hospitals account for 81.8% of the city's modeled trauma catchment.

Temporal Patterns

Shootings peaked in 2021 at 2,338 incidents (81.8% above 2015 baseline), coinciding with pandemic-era disruptions. Violence has since declined 60% from the 2021 peak.
